Overview

The goal of this research is to create a novel co-design framework for the integration of dynamic humanoid robots with electric motors. This new framework will consider how the robot hardware and control jointly affects the performance of demanding physical tasks. The adoption of powerful electric motors has recently revolutionized quadrupedal robots, transforming them from research platforms into useful tools in real world applications. To date, these technologies have not been translated to humanoids, as their control and hardware designs are substantially more complex and intertwined. However, we assume that by formalizing the process of simultaneously designing the robot's actuation and control, we can leverage powerful electric motors to create humanoid robots with athletic abilities comparable to those of humans.

This research will enable the integration of this transformative actuation paradigm into humanoid robots through advances across the design, simulation, and control that are tailored to these platforms. Novel fundamental performance metrics and design principles specific to dynamic humanoid robots will be leveraged to enable the optimization of robot hardware and control in a formulation that is scalable to multiple demanding tasks. Since the actuators themselves have non-trivial physical dynamics, actuator dynamics will be integrated into efficient physics simulation pipelines, avoiding speed/accuracy trade-offs present in currently available approaches. Advances to the theory of system identification will be pursued to minimize the sim2real gap resulting from these parallel actuation designs. Finally, a new approach to self-collision avoidance will be embedded into the whole-body controller of humanoid robots to enable robust realization of dynamic motions. These advances will be integrated into a new humanoid DASH, that will experimentally validate the proposed framework by realizing the tasks of sprint running, powerlifting, and carrying heavy objects while walking.

Intellectual Merit

The main Intellectual Merit of this proposal is a holistic framework for the integration of dynamic humanoids that employ electric motors. At the core of this framework is a novel co-design optimization formulation that is scalable to multiple tasks and considers how hardware and control jointly affect robot performance. The transformative nature of this formulation is amplified by three fundamental scientific contributions of this work: (i) Derivation of performance metrics and design principles tailored to dynamic humanoid robots; (ii) creation of efficient computational tools for simulation and identification of the complex mechanisms employed by these robots; and (iii) extension of whole-body control techniques for dynamic motions to prevent issues with self-collision typical of humanoid robots. While these fundamental science aspects are new, it is their integration that represents the ultimate focus and transformative potential of this work. The proposed framework will be validated by the integration of the humanoid robot DASH to perform demanding physical tasks that no existing electric humanoid can yet realize.

Broader Impacts

The creation of humanoid robots with physical capabilities comparable to those of the average human could protect workers that realize demanding physical labor such as firefighters, policemen, delivery personnel, and nurses. For instance, lifting tasks in such professions lead to lower-back injuries for over 600,000 workers each year. The powerful electric motors that transformed quadruped robots into useful tools in the real world present an opportunity to make humanoids a viable alternative for these tasks. To accelerate the dissemination of this transformative actuation paradigm into the community, the robot DASH constructed in this project will be open-sourced to lower the barrier to entry for research with humanoid robots. To facilitate adoption by other groups, we target the creation of a capable and versatile platform that is also low-cost, safe, and practical. The lessons learned from this research will be reflected in education through the creation of a course on the design and control of dynamic robots co-taught between the PI's universities. Students will use an open-source robotics kit to co-design their own hopping leg, culminating in a friendly robot race between Universities.
